Research on negative point systems in schools

Please read the summaries below before enabling this feature. Your acknowledgement is logged.

Manolev, Sullivan & Slee (2019) — The datafication of discipline: ClassDojo, surveillance and a performative classroom culture — Learning, Media and Technology 44(2)

This Australian study examined how continuous digital behaviour tracking reshapes classroom dynamics. The researchers found that systems with visible negative marks can encourage "performative compliance" — students behaving to avoid losing points rather than developing intrinsic values. Some teachers reported feeling pressure to award or withhold points strategically, which changed the relational texture of their classrooms.

The researchers note that many schools report broadly positive experiences with behaviour apps, and they are not arguing against digital recognition per se. Their critique centres specifically on the public display of negative marks and the use of points as a disciplinary rather than celebratory mechanism. The study calls for schools to make deliberate, evidence-informed decisions about how any such system is configured.

Skinner & Belmont (1993) — Motivation in the classroom — Journal of Educational Psychology 85(4)

This foundational study in motivation psychology identified that external rewards, including point systems, can reduce intrinsic motivation when perceived as controlling. The "overjustification effect" is well-replicated: children who receive rewards for behaviour they already value may disengage from that behaviour when the reward is removed, because the behaviour has been reframed as something done for the reward rather than for its own sake.

Importantly, Skinner and Belmont also found that autonomy-supportive recognition — where acknowledgement is informational and specific rather than controlling — preserves and can enhance intrinsic motivation. The research does not argue against recognition; it argues against recognition that is structured as a control mechanism. Negative marks, by design, are a control mechanism.

DfE — Behaviour in Schools: Advice for headteachers and school staff (February 2024 edition)

The Department for Education's statutory guidance recommends a whole-school approach built around "teach, model, practise, acknowledge." It explicitly supports positive recognition systems and describes them as an important complement to clear, consistent expectations. The guidance does not recommend class-wide point displays that allow children to compare each other's totals, which research links to social comparison anxiety.

The guidance supports point systems used to privately celebrate individual effort and communicate specifically with parents. Where schools choose to use negative recording mechanisms, it recommends they sit within a broader restorative and relationship-based approach — and that the decision is made deliberately at leadership level, not defaulted into.

Paul Dix — When the Adults Change, Everything Changes (2017, Crown House Publishing)

Dix's widely-read practitioner text argues that sustainable behaviour environments come from consistent adult behaviour and genuine relationships — "private praise before public." He is critical of public negative mark displays and reward ceremonies that leave some children consistently uncelebrated, describing these as undermining the relational work that actually changes behaviour over time.

Dix does not oppose digital recognition tools categorically. His concern is with systems that create visible comparisons between children, or that use negative marks as a primary behaviour management mechanism. His alternative — specific, private, relational recognition — is precisely what Wren Points is designed to do by default. Schools that want negative points can enable them; this gate exists to ensure the decision is made with awareness of the evidence.
